The HJM Equation

The HJM framework models the instantaneous forward rate f(t,T) for all maturities T simultaneously:

df(t,T) = α(t,T) dt + σ(t,T) dW(t)

Under the risk-neutral measure, the drift is fully determined by the volatility:

DRIFT RESTRICTION α(t,T) = σ(t,T) ∫tT σ(t,s) ds
Key Insight

Once you specify the volatility function σ(t,T), the drift α(t,T) is completely pinned down by no-arbitrage. You have no freedom to choose the drift independently. This is the central result of HJM.

Why Does the Drift Restriction Matter?

1Start with forward rates

The forward rate f(t,T) determines bond prices: P(t,T) = exp(-∫ f(t,s) ds). Any model of forward rates implicitly models bonds.

2Require no-arbitrage

For a market free of arbitrage, discounted bond prices must be martingales under the risk-neutral measure. This is a consistency condition.

3Derive the drift

Applying Ito's lemma to P(t,T) and enforcing the martingale condition yields the drift restriction: α = σ · ∫σ. No free parameters remain.

4Model = volatility choice

The entire HJM model reduces to choosing σ(t,T). Different choices recover known models: constant σ gives Ho-Lee, exponential decay gives Hull-White.

HJM Special Cases

Different volatility specifications recover well-known short rate models:

Ho-Lee Model
σ(t,T) = σ0

Constant volatility. Produces normally distributed rates with no mean reversion. Forward curves shift in parallel with a drift term.

Hull-White Model
σ(t,T) = σ0 e-a(T-t)

Exponentially decaying volatility. Generates mean-reverting short rates. Near-term forward rates are more volatile than distant ones.

Ritchken-Sankarasubramanian
σ(t,T) = σ0(T-t)e-a(T-t)

Humped volatility. Captures the empirical observation that intermediate maturities have the highest volatility.
